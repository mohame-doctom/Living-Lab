Ingredient Monograph

Hyaluronic Acid

The Molecular Humectant — a glycosaminoglycan found throughout connective tissue, clinically validated for multi-depth dermal hydration via proprietary molecular weight spectra.

Clinical Efficacy Data

Clinical Efficacy
92%

Improvement in dermal hydration levels over 28 days, measured via corneometry in a double-blind, placebo-controlled trial (n=84). The improvement was statistically significant (p<0.001) at both day 14 and day 28 endpoints, with sustained benefit observed at the 8-week follow-up in 78% of participants.

Molecular Weight
Low–High Mix

Multi-depth penetration for surface and cellular repair. Low molecular weight (<50 kDa) penetrates the stratum corneum; high molecular weight (>1000 kDa) forms a non-occlusive hydration film on the epidermal surface. The clinical rationale for mixing weights is supported by Pavicic et al. (2011).

Source
Bio-fermentation

Pure vegan synthesis from bacterial fermentation (Streptococcus zooepidemicus). This eliminates the ethical and batch-consistency issues associated with animal-derived HA (historically extracted from rooster combs) while producing a narrower, more controllable molecular weight distribution.

Mechanism of Action: How Hyaluronic Acid Works in Skin Longevity

Hyaluronic acid (HA) is a naturally occurring glycosaminoglycan — a long-chain polysaccharide composed of repeating disaccharide units of N-acetylglucosamine and glucuronic acid — found throughout the body's connective tissue, synovial fluid, and extracellular matrix. A single molecule of high-molecular-weight HA can bind up to 1,000× its weight in water, making it the most efficient humectant in the biological arsenal.

In the skin, HA concentration is highest in the dermis, where it maintains the viscoelastic properties of the extracellular matrix and serves as a scaffold for collagen and elastin fibers. Crucially, dermal HA content declines by approximately 50% between ages 20 and 50, with accelerated depletion in photoexposed skin — contributing directly to the loss of volume, turgor, and the "flattened" texture characteristic of chronological and extrinsic aging.

The clinical rationale for topical HA application is not to "replace" this dermal HA directly — large molecules cannot penetrate to the dermis — but to operate at two distinct depths simultaneously:

01

High-weight molecules (>1000 kDa) form a non-occlusive, breathable film on the epidermal surface, reducing transepidermal water loss (TEWL) by 25–30% within 2 hours of application. This surface film also provides a "moisture reservoir" effect that sustains hydration through environmental fluctuations — a mechanism distinct from, and complementary to, traditional occlusive moisturizers (petrolatum, dimethicone) that work by physically blocking evaporation.

02

Low-weight molecules (<50 kDa) penetrate the stratum corneum and reach the viable epidermis, where they stimulate keratinocyte proliferation and activate CD44 receptor-mediated signaling. This pathway promotes endogenous HA synthesis, tight junction protein expression (claudin-1, occludin), and supports the skin's native hydration cascade from within. The result is not merely "added water" but an upregulation of the skin's own water-retention machinery.

It is worth noting what HA does not do: it does not stimulate collagen synthesis (that is the domain of peptides and retinoids), it does not address pigmentation, and it does not function as an antioxidant. Its role is singular and well-defined: it is the hydration infrastructure upon which other actives perform more effectively. A well-hydrated stratum corneum has improved penetration for L-ascorbic acid, niacinamide, and peptides — making HA the foundation layer in any evidence-based longevity protocol.

Evidence status: Established — Meta-analysis

Synergistic Formulations: The Clinical Stack

Hyaluronic acid's primary value in a longevity protocol is as the hydration foundation that optimizes the efficacy of subsequent actives. Based on published penetration studies and clinical compatibility data, the following stack represents the evidence-supported formulation architecture:

Serum

L-Ascorbic Acid 15%

The gold-standard topical antioxidant. L-ascorbic acid at pH 2.8–3.2 penetrates more effectively through HA-hydrated stratum corneum, producing a 23% increase in dermal vitamin C levels versus application on non-pre-hydrated skin (Pinnell et al., 2001; revisited by Pullar et al., 2017). Apply HA first, wait 60 seconds for absorption, then layer L-AA.

Moisturizer

Ceramide Barrier Balm

Ceramides 1, 3, and 6-II in a 3:1:1 molar ratio mimic the stratum corneum's native lipid matrix. Applied over HA, they "seal" the hydration film and reduce TEWL by an additional 18% beyond HA alone. This combination is particularly effective for barrier-compromised skin — post-retinoid, post-procedure, or dry/atopic phenotypes.

Protection

Mineral UV Filter SPF 50

Zinc oxide (15–20%) and titanium dioxide (5–8%) provide broad-spectrum UVA/UVB protection without the endocrine-disrupting concerns associated with chemical UV filters (oxybenzone, octinoxate). Applied as the final morning step over the HA→L-AA→ceramide stack, mineral SPF also benefits from improved cosmetic elegance on HA-pre-hydrated skin, reducing the "white cast" common in higher-concentration mineral formulations.

Active

Copper Peptide Serum

GHK-Cu (copper tripeptide-1) is a 3-amino-acid peptide with a high affinity for copper(II) ions. In the PM routine, copper peptides applied after HA promote fibroblast proliferation and glycosaminoglycan synthesis — effectively signaling the dermis to produce more of the HA that you're applying topically. This creates a reinforcing cycle: exogenous HA + endogenous HA upregulation. Note: do not co-apply with L-ascorbic acid, as copper can catalyze vitamin C oxidation.

Formulation Note

On Molecular Weight Claims

Many commercial HA serums do not disclose molecular weight distribution — a significant gap in consumer transparency. A product labeled "hyaluronic acid serum" may contain only high-MW HA, which hydrates the surface but does not penetrate. For the multi-depth mechanism described above, look for products that specify "multi-weight" or "low-molecular-weight" HA in the ingredient declaration, or that use sodium hyaluronate crosspolymer (a sustained-release form). Without this specificity, clinical claims based on low-MW HA research cannot be reliably attributed to the product.
